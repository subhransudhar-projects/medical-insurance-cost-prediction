Executive Summary

The story in one screen

The problem, the approach, the findings, and the action — then the evidence follows.

The problem. Health insurers must predict what a customer will cost to price policies fairly, flag high-risk members, and target wellness spend. Mispricing loses customers or loses money.

The approach. A four-stage pipeline — cleaning → exploratory analysis → a 58-figure visualization suite → 17 tuned models with SHAP explainability — built with strict leakage control and honest cross-validation.

The findings. Smoking dominates cost, and it compounds with obesity: an obese smoker averages $41,558 versus ~$8,400 for any non-smoker. Age is a steady secondary driver; region and sex are not reliable drivers at all.

The action. Reprice on a smoker×BMI risk tier, fund smoking cessation, and target weight management at smokers specifically — a conservative ~$15.3M/year opportunity per 10,000 members.

Chapter 1

The Data

1,337 policyholders, seven columns, zero missing values after cleaning.

Each record holds a person's age, sex, bmi, number of children, smoker status, region, and their annual medical charges (the target). The portfolio is typical: ~20.5% smokers, a near 50/50 sex split, and an even regional spread. One exact duplicate was removed; no values were imputed.

The target — charges — is heavily right-skewed: most people are inexpensive, but a long tail of high-cost members drives the mean well above the median. That shape is the first clue that a small group accounts for a large share of total cost.

Chapter 2

What Drives Costs — The Findings

Five findings, each confirmed by multiple independent methods and shown with the charts that produced it.

Finding 1

Smoking dominates every other factor

Smokers average $32,050 versus $8,441 for non-smokers — a 3.8× gap and the single strongest signal in the data. Smokers are just 20.5% of members but account for roughly half of all claims cost. No other variable comes close.

Finding 2 · the key insight

Smoking × obesity compounds — it multiplies, it doesn't add

A flat "smoker surcharge" misses the real story. Among non-smokers, BMI barely affects cost. Among smokers, charges climb sharply past the obesity threshold (BMI 30): an obese smoker averages $41,558 versus $21,363 for a non-obese smoker — while an obese non-smoker pays just $8,856, almost the same as any non-smoker. This interaction is what separates a good cost model from a naive one.

Finding 3

Age is a steady secondary driver

Charges rise about $267/year (~$2,700 per decade) for smokers and non-smokers alike. Age matters — but the smoker gap stays wide across every age band rather than closing, so age never rivals smoking.

Finding 4 · a negative result

Region is not a reliable driver

The Southeast looks nominally highest, but the difference fails variance-robust significance tests (Welch ANOVA p≈0.05, Kruskal–Wallis p≈0.20). Its slight edge tracks higher average BMI, not geography. Reporting this honestly — rather than over-claiming a regional effect — is part of the rigor.

Finding 5 · a negative result

Sex is not a driver

There is no practically meaningful difference between male and female charges (Cohen's d ≈ 0.12) and no meaningful interaction with other variables. Both region and sex are excluded as rating factors — for fairness and because they simply aren't predictive.

Relationships at a glance

Zooming out to all variables at once confirms the hierarchy: the smoking signal (and its interactions with BMI and age) carries almost all of the correlation with cost.

Chapter 3

The Modeling Approach

17 configurations, tuned with grid search and honestly cross-validated.

I built and compared linear models, tree ensembles, SVR, KNN, and stacked/voting ensembles — each tuned with GridSearchCV over 5-fold cross-validation, with target-encoded features refit train-only to prevent leakage. Scale-sensitive models were wrapped in per-fold pipelines.

Why Gradient Boosting, and why R² 0.85 not 0.92

The top six models tie within noise (CV-mean spread 0.008 vs CV std 0.040), so I chose the model that is accurate, simple, and natively explainable rather than a marginally-higher ensemble. And I quote the cross-validated R² ≈ 0.85 — not the flattering single-split 0.92 — because I measured that split's optimism directly (+0.076). Honest numbers beat impressive ones.

Are the residuals well-behaved?

The diagnostics are clean: residuals are roughly centred with no strong structure, and the largest errors are concentrated in high-cost non-smokers — people whose true cost drivers (chronic conditions, procedures) are simply absent from a seven-column dataset. That gap becomes a data-acquisition recommendation.

Chapter 4

Model Interpretation — SHAP

Explainable AI: every prediction traced to dollars.

SHAP assigns each feature a signed dollar contribution to every prediction. Smoking-related features account for 62% of the model's predictive weight; age ~11%; region and sex combined just 3.6%. The single most important feature, smoker_bmi, swings an individual estimate by up to +$25,000, with a visible step-change at BMI 30.

Chapter 5

Business Recommendations & Impact

Insights are useless without action.

  • Reprice on a smoker×BMI tier, not a flat surcharge — today's approach under-prices obese smokers by ~$20,000 each and over-prices lean smokers.
  • Fund smoking cessation (largest addressable cost pool) and target weight management at smokers specifically — where it actually pays off.
  • Acquire claims / diagnosis data — the model's worst misses are high-cost non-smokers whose drivers aren't in the current seven columns.
  • Deploy transparently with per-quote SHAP explanations; exclude sex and region as rating factors (fairness + they aren't predictive).
Estimated impact (per 10,000 members, conservative)

Risk-based repricing ~$13.1M (premium adequacy) + smoking cessation ~$1.39M + targeted weight management ~$0.78M  =  ~$15.3M/year (~$1,531/member). Repricing is a premium-adequacy correction, kept distinct from claims reduction rather than blurred into one inflated "savings" figure.

Chapter 6

Technical Appendix

What makes these numbers trustworthy — and where they stop.

Methodology & rigor
  • Leakage control: target-encoded features (smoker_mean_charges, region_mean_charges) refit on the training fold only; within-CV leak impact measured at 0.0002.
  • Honest evaluation: stratified 80/20 split on smoker, 5-fold CV, and the +0.076 test-split optimism measured and reported rather than hidden.
  • Fair comparison: scale-sensitive models (SVR, KNN) wrapped in per-fold StandardScaler pipelines; SVR's target scaled via TransformedTargetRegressor (R² 0.36 → 0.92).
  • Reproducible: every stage seeded (random_state=42), modular scripts, a JSON results registry, and persisted models + splits.
Limitations & honest caveats
  • Seven features can't capture chronic conditions, diagnoses, or procedures — the source of the largest residual errors.
  • Ensembles merely tied the best single model (base learners make correlated errors on the same smoker signal); this is reported, not overstated.
  • Published notebooks citing R² ≈ 0.94 did not replicate under leakage-controlled CV — likely single-split luck and/or target leakage.
  • The "insurance company" is an illustrative framing to structure the analysis, not a real named client.

Conclusion

One in five members drives half the cost; smoking-and-obesity is the engine. The business can now price, target, and explain that risk — accurately, honestly, and profitably. The model is good (CV R² ≈ 0.85), but the more valuable output is the understanding: a defensible, explainable account of exactly where cost comes from and what to do about it.
